\section{Introduction and Motivation}
In various real-world machine learning contexts, a single underlying environment is observed through multiple views, often spanning different modalities. These distinct views are generated by a complex mixture of hidden causal factors. Some of these latent factors are shared across the views (the ``content"), while others are entirely specific to a single modality (the ``style"). Recovering this shared content is a primary goal of representation learning, as it allows us to discard irrelevant, view-specific noise and isolate the robust, causal variables that define the true state of the environment.

Recently, Yao et al.\ introduced a comprehensive framework in \textit{Multi-View Causal Representation Learning with Partial Observability} \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}. Their work establishes two fundamental theoretical guarantees. First, they prove that the shared content of a single set of views can be \textit{block-identified}, provided the exact dimensionality of this shared content is known prior to training. Second, they demonstrate that by introducing a learnable masking mechanism and evaluating the shared content across all possible subsets of views simultaneously, one can identify the shared content of any subset without the need to retrain encoders for each specific combination. Crucially, this masking approach also overcomes the restrictive assumption of needing to know the exact content dimension beforehand.

While theoretically powerful, evaluating all possible view subsets simultaneously becomes computationally intensive as the number of views increases.
This complexity is often unnecessary in practice, where interest is typically restricted to the global content—the latent variables shared across all views—rather than every possible intersection.
Furthermore, while the masking approach of Yao et al.\ \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning} addresses the challenge of an unknown content dimension, their framework assumes that the dimension of the latent space generating each individual view is known, which is rarely the case in real-world applications.
Finally, their proposed information-sharing regularizer used to learn the masks relies on an $L_0$ penalty, which is highly non-convex and challenging to optimize in practice.

As such, in section \ref{sec:method}, we propose solutions to these challenges by (i) restricting the focus to global content recovery to eliminate combinatorial overhead, (ii) relaxing the requirement for known view-specific dimensions by only requiring overestimation of the content dimension, and (iii) utilizing the differentiable hard concrete distribution \cite{louizos2018learningsparseneuralnetworks} to efficiently optimize the latent masks.

\section{Problem Setup and Notation} \label{sec:setup}
We formalize the environment by assuming the existence of ground-truth latent variables $\mathbf{z} = (\mathbf{z}_1, \dots, \mathbf{z}_N)$ sampled from a distribution $p_\mathbf{z}$. Instead of observing $\mathbf{z}$ directly, we have access to a set of $K$ distinct views, denoted as $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_K)$. Crucially, each view $\mathbf{x}_k$ is only a partial observation of the environment. It is generated by a specific subset of the latent variables, $\mathbf{z}_{S_k}$, where the indices are defined by a subset $S_k \subseteq [N]=\{1, \dots, N\}$. The generation of each view is governed by a view-specific, smooth, and invertible mixing function $f_k$ mapping to an observation space $\mathcal{X}_k \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{\text{dim}(\mathbf{x}_k)}$, such that $\mathbf{x}_k := f_k(\mathbf{z}_{S_k})$. See Fig. \ref{fig:setup} for an illustration of the setup.

Let $\mathcal{V} := \{V \subseteq [K] : |V| \ge 2\}$ denote the collection of subsets $V \in \mathcal{V}$ indexing two or more views.
For $V \in \mathcal{V}$, we denote the subset of views as $x_V := \{x_k : k \in V\}$. The corresponding shared content indices are given by the intersection of the view-specific indexing sets:
\begin{equation*}
  C_V = \bigcap_{k \in V} S_k
\end{equation*}
The associated shared latent variables are $\mathbf{z}_{C_V} := \{\mathbf{z}_j : j \in C_V\}$.
Our work focuses exclusively on the global content, defined as the latent variables shared across the complete set of observed views (i.e., where $V = [K]$).

To facilitate the identification of these shared variables through learnable masks, we define a selection operator $\oslash$. For a binary mask $\mathbf{a} \in \{0, 1\}^d$ and a vector $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the operator is defined as:
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{a} \oslash \mathbf{b} := [\mathbf{b}_j : \mathbf{a}_j = 1, j \in [d]].
\end{equation*}

The primary objective of our framework—reflecting a prominent line of inquiry within multi-view causal representation learning—is to utilize the observed views to train encoders that extract all, and only, the information contained in the shared latent variables. In this context, the successful recovery of shared information is formally defined in terms of \textit{block-identifiability}.
\begin{definition}[Block-Identifiability]
  The true content variables $\mathbf{c}$ are block-identified by a function $g: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{\text{dim}(\mathbf{c})}$ if the inferred content partition $\hat{\mathbf{c}} = g(\mathbf{x})$ contains all and only information about $\mathbf{c}$. That is, there exists some smooth invertible mapping $h$ such that $\hat{\mathbf{c}} = h(\mathbf{c})$.
\end{definition}

\section{Theoretical Foundations} \label{sec:TheorGuar}
Our approach is inspired by the identifiability results of Yao et al. \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}, which we present below.
Their theoretical guarantees require the following assumptions:
\begin{assumption}[General Assumptions] \label{genAsm}
  For the generative model defined in Section \ref{sec:setup}:
  (i) Each view-specific mixing function $f_k$ is a diffeomorphism;
  (ii) $p_\mathbf{z}$ is a smooth and continuous density with $p_\mathbf{z} > 0$ almost everywhere.
\end{assumption}

Their first result demonstrates that for any fixed subset $V \in \mathcal{V}$, the shared content is block-identifiable using \textit{content encoders} $g_k$.

\begin{theorem}[Identifiability from a Fixed Set of Views \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}] \label{thm:contBlock}
  Under Asm.\ \ref{genAsm}, let $G = \{g_k\}_{k \in V}$ be a set of content encoders that minimizes:
  \begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(G) = \sum_{k < k' \in V} \mathbb{E} \left[ \|g_k(\mathbf{x}_k) - g_{k'}(\mathbf{x}_{k'})\|_2 \right] - \sum_{k \in V} H(g_k(\mathbf{x}_k))
  \end{equation}
  where $H(\cdot)$ denotes differential entropy and the expectation is over $p(\mathbf{x}_V)$. Then $\mathbf{z}_{C_V}$ is block-identified by $g_k \in G$ for any $k \in V$.
\end{theorem}

To avoid retraining encoders for every subset, their second result introduces learned \textit{content selectors} (masks) and projections to achieve simultaneous identifiability across all $V \in \mathcal{V}$ using shared \textit{view-specific encoders}.

\begin{theorem}[Simultaneous Identifiability via Masking \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}] \label{thm:allContBlocks}
  Under Asm.\ \ref{genAsm}, let view-specific encoders $R = \{r_k\}$, content selectors $\Phi = \{\phi_V^k\}$, and projections $T = \{t_k\}$ solve:
  \begin{equation}
    \min \text{Reg}(\Phi) \quad \text{subject to:} \quad R, \Phi, T \in \arg\min \mathcal{L}(R, \Phi, T)
  \end{equation}
  where $\text{Reg}(\Phi)$ is an $L_0$ sparsity penalty on the selectors, and the base loss is:
  \begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(R, \Phi, T) = \sum_{V \in \mathcal{V}} \sum_{k < k' \in V} \mathbb{E} \left[ \|\phi_V^k \oslash r_k(\mathbf{x}_k) - \phi_V^{k'} \oslash r_{k'}(\mathbf{x}_{k'})\|_2 \right] - \sum_{k \in V} H(t_k \circ r_k(\mathbf{x}_k))
  \end{equation}
  Then for any $V \in \mathcal{V}$ and $k \in V$, the masked representation $\phi_V^k \oslash r_k$ block-identifies $\mathbf{z}_{C_V}$.
\end{theorem}

Formal definitions and extended intuition for the encoders, selectors, and projections are deferred to Appendices \ref{app:definitions} and \ref{app:intuition}.

\begin{comment}
\subsection{Computational Advantage of Global Content Recovery}
A key motivation for restricting attention to the global shared content block is computational simplicity. In the full framework of Yao et al., one considers a family $\mathcal{V}$ of subsets of views and learns a selector $\phi_V^k$ for each subset-view pair. Although the number of encoders remains linear in the number of views, the number of subset-specific alignment constraints grows rapidly with the size of $\mathcal{V}$. In the worst case, when all nontrivial subsets are considered, this introduces a combinatorial burden in both optimization and interpretation.

By contrast, in our global-only setting, we optimize only the single intersection
\[
C = \bigcap_{k \in V} S_k.
\]
As a result, each view requires only one global selector, and the training objective only needs to enforce agreement among the masked representations corresponding to this single shared block. This removes the need to separately model content shared by intermediate subsets of views, yielding a substantially simpler objective and a more scalable implementation.

This simplification is especially attractive in settings where the primary downstream goal is to recover the semantic core common to all modalities, rather than to resolve every partially shared latent structure. In this sense, our framework trades representational granularity for computational efficiency and interpretability.
\end{comment}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
We focus on recovering the global shared content $\mathbf{z}_{C_{[K]}}$ by aligning representations across all $K$ views. Unlike previous theoretical frameworks that require known content or view-specific latent dimensions, we operate under a relaxed setting where all view-specific encoders $r_k$ map to a unified latent space of dimension $d$. We assume only that this common dimension $d$ is an overestimation of the true content dimension.

To bridge the gap between theory and practice, we introduce two primary modifications. First, we implement a \textit{shared masking mechanism} where a single binary mask $\phi \in \{0, 1\}^d$ is applied globally across the outputs of all encoders $\{r_k\}_{k=1}^K$. This enforces a common latent subspace for shared content and significantly simplifies the per-view selectors required by Theorem \ref{thm:allContBlocks}. We optimize this mask end-to-end using a \textit{hard concrete distribution} \cite{louizos2018learningsparseneuralnetworks}, which enables differentiable $L_0$ regularization while yielding exact binary values to prune unshared dimensions.

Second, we replace the intractable differential entropy terms in the theoretical objective with a \textit{Symmetric InfoNCE} loss \cite{oord2019representationlearningcontrastivepredictive, radford2021learning}. This self-supervised surrogate enforces alignment through pairwise contrast across all views while preventing dimensional collapse by maximizing a lower bound on mutual information. The total objective is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SymInfoNCE}} + \lambda \text{Reg}(\phi).
\end{equation}
Detailed formulations of the masking distribution and the contrastive objective are provided in Appendix \ref{app:approach_details}.

\section{Experiments}

Our experiments follow the same flavor as in \cite{daunhawer2023identifiabilityresultsmultimodalcontrastive} and \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}: we first evaluate the performance of our method on synthetic data, where we have access to the ground truth latent variables. Then, we apply our method to the \textit{Multimodal3DIdent} dataset \cite{daunhawer2023identifiabilityresultsmultimodalcontrastive}, which consists of blender-rendered images of 3D objects, paired with descriptive text captions.
The code is available in our Github repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Virusmint/multiview-sparse}} and builds upon the code of Daunhawer et al. \cite{daunhawer2023identifiabilityresultsmultimodalcontrastive} and Yao et al. \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning}.

To evaluate block-identifiability, we assess whether the learned representations capture all and only the information contained in the shared content. We quantify this by training downstream predictors to recover the ground-truth latent factors directly from the learned embeddings. Successful block identification is characterized by high predictive performance for shared content variables and near-chance performance for style variables, except in cases where significant statistical dependencies exist. Specifically, we report the $R^2$ scores from linear regression for continuous latent factors and the classification accuracy from multinomial logistic regression for discrete factors.

\section{Implementation: Global Content Recovery}
Our implementation focuses on recovering the global shared content $z_C$ by optimizing the alignment of masked representations from each view. To handle the unknown dimensionality of this global content block and ensure the identifiability of individual causal factors, we integrate two key mechanisms: the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation \cite{jang2017categoricalreparameterizationgumbelsoftmax} and the Barlow Twins objective \cite{pmlr-v139-zbontar21a}.

\subsection{Gumbel-Softmax for Mask Learning}
In Theorem 3.8, the content selectors $\Phi$ are defined as discrete binary masks. However, optimizing a discrete $L_0$ penalty is non-differentiable. To allow for end-to-end training via gradient descent, we utilize the Gumbel-Softmax (or Gumbel-Sigmoid) reparameterization trick \cite{jang2017categoricalreparameterizationgumbelsoftmax}.

We represent each mask entry as a learnable logit $\alpha^{(k)}_j$. During the forward pass, we sample noise from a Gumbel distribution to produce a continuous relaxation of the binary selection:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\phi}^{(k)}_j = \sigma \left( \frac{\alpha^{(k)}_j + G_1 - G_0}{\tau} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, $\tau$ is the temperature, and the $G_i$ are independent standard Gumbel noises, which can be generated from uniform noise $U_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ via $G_i = -\log(-\log(U_i))$. As $\tau \to 0$, the output approaches a hard binary mask. This allows the network to "softly" explore which latent dimensions contribute to global alignment during early training and converge to a sparse, hard selection of content variables.
After training, we obtain strictly binary hard masks by applying indicators to the soft masks: $\phi^{(k)}_j = \mathbbm{1}(\tilde{\phi}^{(k)}_j > 0.5)$.
\end{comment}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
While our empirical results are strong, this work lacks formal proofs for the relaxed setting in which the only assumption is the overestimation of the content dimension.
Future research should establish the theoretical conditions required for block-identifiability under these assumptions.
Additionally, the impact of the degree of overestimation on recovery accuracy and training cost remains to be explored. Investigating the consequences of underestimation would also be an avenue of practical relevance for our approach.

\begin{comment}
While the global-only formulation offers a substantial reduction in computational complexity, it also introduces an important limitation: it does not explicitly recover latent factors shared by only a subset of views. In applications where partially shared structure is semantically meaningful, this simplification may discard useful information.

A second limitation is that our implementation is motivated by the identifiability theory but does not itself constitute a new identifiability proof for the hard-concrete-plus-Barlow-Twins training objective. Rather, it should be interpreted as a practical approximation to the alignment and entropy principles underlying the theoretical framework.

Several directions remain for future work. First, the proposed objective should be evaluated empirically against the original subset-based framework on synthetic multi-view data with controlled overlap patterns. Second, one could study whether intermediate levels of sharing can be incorporated without recovering the full combinatorial cost of all-subset modeling. Finally, it would be valuable to investigate whether alternative entropy-promoting objectives could provide a closer match to the assumptions appearing in the identifiability theory.
\end{comment}

\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we addressed the challenge of bridging the gap between theoretical identifiability and practical implementation in multi-view representation learning. Specifically, we introduced a differentiable shared masking mechanism that relaxes the restrictive requirement for prior knowledge of the latent space's exact dimensionality. By allowing for an overestimation of the content dimension, our approach provides a more robust and realistic framework for recovering global shared content. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that our method achieves performance comparable to previous works \cite{daunhawer2023identifiabilityresultsmultimodalcontrastive, yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning} that rely on oracle knowledge of latent dimensionality.

\section{Definitions for Section \ref{sec:TheorGuar}} \label{app:definitions}
\begin{definition}[Content Encoders]
  Assuming the content size $|C|$ is given for a set of jointly observed views $x_V$, the content encoders $G := \{g_k : \mathcal{X}_k \rightarrow (0, 1)^{|C|}\}_{k \in V}$ consist of smooth functions mapping the respective observation spaces to the $|C|$-dimensional unit cube.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[View-Specific Encoders]
  The view-specific encoders $R := \{r_k : \mathcal{X}_k \rightarrow \mathcal{Z}_{S_k}\}_{k \in V}$ consist of smooth functions mapping from the respective observation spaces to the view-specific latent space, where the dimension of the $k$-th latent space $|S_k|$ is assumed to be known.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Content Selectors]
  The content selectors $\Phi := \{\phi_V^k : V \in \mathcal{V}, k \in V\}$ are learnable binary vectors, with $\phi_V^k \in \{0, 1\}^{|S_k|}$, that perform element-wise selection (denoted by $\oslash$) on the encoded representation such that $\phi_V^k \oslash r_k(x_k)$ extracts the selected representation.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Projections]
  The set of projections $T := \{t_k\}_{k \in V}$ consist of smooth functions $t_k : \mathcal{Z}_{S_k} \rightarrow (0, 1)^{|S_k|}$ mapping each view-specific latent space to a hyper unit-cube of the same dimension $|S_k|$. These enforce invertibility of the encoders without interfering with disentanglement.
\end{definition}

\section{Intuition for Mathematical Components} \label{app:intuition}

\subsection{Content Selectors ($\Phi$)}
The content selectors act as a ``soft index" or a routing mechanism. Since the encoder $r_k$ does not know a priori which dimensions of its output will match the dimensions of another view's encoder, the selectors $\phi$ learn to point to the correct coordinates. The information-sharing regularizer $\text{Reg}(\Phi)$ acts as an Occam's Razor: it pushes the model to explain the correlation between views using the fewest number of latent dimensions possible. Without this regularizer, the model might trivially designate all dimensions as ``shared", failing to distinguish between universal content and view-specific style.

\subsection{Projections ($T$)}
The projection functions $t_k$ are introduced to resolve a conflict between invertibility and causal dependency. Standard contrastive learning often forces representations to be independent and uniform (to maximize entropy). However, if the true underlying causal variables are dependent, they cannot be independent.

The projection $t_k$ acts as a buffer. By maximizing the entropy of the \textit{projected} representation $t_k(r_k(x_k))$, the framework ensures the mapping $r_k$ is invertible (preserving all information) without forcing the latent variables themselves to be independent. This allows the model to correctly identify variables that have causal links to one another while still satisfying the mathematical requirements for a non-degenerate solution.

\section{Simultaneous Learning of Content Blocks}
A core contribution of Yao et al.\ \cite{yao2024multiviewcausalrepresentationlearning} is the ability to learn shared content across all possible subsets of views without an exponential explosion in model parameters. In a naive setup, one might assume that identifying content for every subset $V_i \subseteq V$ requires $2^K$ separate encoders.

However, the authors formulate the problem such that each view $k$ is associated with exactly one encoder $r_k$. The latent space of this encoder is assumed to contain all variables relevant to that view, including both style and various levels of shared content. To isolate specific content blocks, they apply different binary masks $\phi_V^k$ to the \textit{same} latent representation $r_k(x_k)$. Because these masks are simply vectors of parameters, the number of encoders scales linearly ($O(K)$) with the number of views, even as the number of potential content intersections scales exponentially. In our work, we further simplify this by maintaining the single-encoder structure but only optimizing for the single mask corresponding to the global intersection $C$.

\section{Detailed Approach and Formulations} \label{app:approach_details}

\subsection{Hard Concrete Masking}
The discrete $L_0$ penalty is non-differentiable. Following \cite{louizos2018learningsparseneuralnetworks}, we treat each entry of the shared mask $\phi_j$ as a stochastic gate sampled from a Bernoulli distribution. To enable gradient-based optimization, we use the hard concrete distribution to ``stretch'' a continuous variable $s$ into the interval $(\gamma, \zeta)$, where $\gamma < 0$ and $\zeta > 1$. The mask $\tilde{\phi}_j$ is obtained via a hard-sigmoid:
\begin{equation}
  s = \text{Sigmoid} \left( \frac{\log u - \log(1-u) + \log \alpha_j}{\beta} \right), \quad \tilde{\phi}_j = \min(1, \max(0, s(\zeta - \gamma) + \gamma))
\end{equation}
where $u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)$, $\log \alpha_j$ is the location parameter, and $\beta$ is the temperature. This allows the information-sharing regularizer $\text{Reg}(\phi)$ to be implemented as a fully differentiable complexity loss:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Reg}(\phi) = \sum_{j=1}^d \text{Sigmoid}\left(\log \alpha_j - \beta \log \frac{-\gamma}{\zeta}\right).
\end{equation}
At test time, we obtain deterministic masks using the expected estimator:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\phi}_j = \min(1, \max(0, \text{Sigmoid}(\log \alpha_j)(\zeta - \gamma) + \gamma)).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Multiview Symmetric InfoNCE}
Let $\hat{z}_k = \tilde{\phi} \oslash r_k(x_k)$ be the masked representation for view $k$. To ensure joint alignment across all $K$ views, we employ a Symmetric InfoNCE loss \cite{oord2019representationlearningcontrastivepredictive, radford2021learning}, aggregated across all unique pairs $(k, k')$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{SymInfoNCE}} = \frac{1}{K(K-1)B} \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{k' \neq k} \sum_{i=1}^B -\log \frac{e^{\text{sim}(\hat{z}_{i,k}, \hat{z}_{i,k'})/\tau}}{\sum_{j=1}^B e^{\text{sim}(\hat{z}_{i,k}, \hat{z}_{j,k'})/\tau}}
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the batch size, $\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ denotes the similarity measure (e.g., negative Euclidean distance or cosine similarity) and $\tau$ is the temperature parameter. The denominator acts as a surrogate for entropy maximization by contrasting paired representations against batch distractors, preventing the trivial solution of dimensional collapse.

\subsection{Experimental Details} \label{app:ExpDetails}

\paragraph{Multimodal Experiment with Multimodal3DIdent} The Multimodal3DIdent dataset \cite{daunhawer2023identifiabilityresultsmultimodalcontrastive} consists of $224 \times 224 \times 3$ RGB images paired with descriptive natural language captions. The images are rendered in Blender using 10 underlying factors: object shape (7 classes), x and y positions (3 discrete values each), 3D rotations (3 continuous angles), object colour, spotlight position, spotlight colour, and background colour. The corresponding text is generated from 5 factors: shape, x-position, y-position, colour names selected from random palettes, and one of five unique sentence templates that determine the phrasing style. Examples of the dataset are displayed in Fig. \ref{fig:multi_example}

The generative process is designed so that only three factors—object shape, x-position, and y-position—are identical across both modalities. While both views reference the object's colour, the image uses continuous hue values while the text uses discrete names from varying palettes, preventing a direct one-to-one correspondence. All other factors, such as camera rotation, lighting, and sentence structure, are modality-specific.
